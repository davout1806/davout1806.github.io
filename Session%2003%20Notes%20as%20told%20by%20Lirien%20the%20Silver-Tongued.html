The Ballad of Frandor’s Keep: Secrets in the Stone and Shadows on the Road

By Lirien the Silver-Tongued

The Depths of the Old Mine

Beneath the earth, where time itself seemed to slow and the air carried the acrid scent of decay, our heroes ventured deeper into the abandoned mine. The rock whispered secrets, the walls bore scars of pick and hammer long abandoned, and the weight of history pressed upon them.

It was Gwador who first caught the foul stench—like rotten eggs left too long in the sun. A warning, perhaps, of treacherous air rising from fissures in the stone. But danger came not from the air, but from men.

Two figures lurked in the gloom—bandits, their eyes wide with fear. They had taken refuge here after slaying two of Rikar’s Ravens, and now, as the heroes approached, panic set in. “You’re with Rikar, ain’t ya?” one stammered, hands inching toward steel. But no mercy was found in the dark. A swift struggle left them bound and stripped of weapons, their broken crossbows left as useless as their misguided loyalty.

Further in, they found the remnants of those who came before: a corroded winch, a pulley dangling into the abyss, a lantern whose glow had long since faded. Among these relics of toil, they gathered what they could—beaver pelts, a brass mirror once used for signaling, thieves’ tools stained with blood. Flint claimed the tools as his own, Delvan took the lantern, and Gwador grabbed an old miner’s pick.

Then, with careful steps, they descended.

The lower cavern was a twisted labyrinth of time-forgotten corridors, rusted tools fused to the very stone as if the earth itself had devoured them. It was there, in the dark, that a whisper curled through the tunnels like the sigh of a restless soul.

“I was not strong enough… the earth does not forgive the unworthy.”

The words clung to them as they entered a vast chamber, timber beams holding the ceiling aloft, the faint shimmer of metal winking from the walls like buried stars. And on the far side, carved with an artisan’s precision, an inscription bore witness to a name not yet lost to time.

“Strength is not in steel, but in the will to endure. The Earth-Forged shall stand Unbent.” — Draeligor

Flint, silent and still, recognized the name. Draeligor had walked the same path before him, a name whispered in the old legends of his people. But the inscription was recent—no older than a century, its words untouched by decay.

And then, the dead rose.

A skeleton wreathed in violet mist emerged from the shadows, spectral energy crackling in its bony fingers. It loosed its fury upon them, arcane darts of pure death flaring toward Gwador. They fought, steel clashing against bone, magic unraveling the unnatural. When the final blow fell, the thing crumbled to dust, but the echoes of its whispers remained.

  • “The unbent fell. The broken rose.”
  • “He knelt before the trial… but his faith faltered.”
  • “The bracer was his… and yet it was not his to wield.”

Their journey continued, deeper into the mine, through winding tunnels and waterlogged passages. More whispers haunted their steps, shadows of voices long past. And at last, Flint touched the inscription, and the vision struck him like a hammer on anvil.

A warrior knelt before a trial, his hands bound in bracers of the ancients, his soul locked in battle against something vast, something of the earth itself. A trial long abandoned… a lesson yet to be learned.

A Bandit’s Choice and the Hollow Tree’s Secret

Returning to the bandits, Delvan posed a question: had they learned? Their answers were swift and trembling. They had.

Yet the party found themselves unwilling to turn the criminals over to Rikar, nor to the Keep’s justice, which could be as cruel as the lawless roads. Instead, Delvan played the long game. He held out a handful of gold—fool’s gold, iron pyrite taken from the mine—and made them an offer.

“This is what we found below,” he told them. “You can dig for more, or you can go free. Or perhaps, if you are wise, you might offer Rikar a peace offering—tell him of the ‘gold’ that lies below.”

The bandits hesitated. One among them raised a concern. “And how are we supposed to survive? There’s a bear at the entrance.”

And so the party led them to the guardian of the mine, and with careful gestures and quiet words, they sought to forge an understanding between beast and man. Whether the bear understood… well, that remained to be seen.

Back on the road, they marked the old hollow tree they had sought for some time. It was said to be a place of appeasement, where dark spirits and forgotten gods demanded gold. A mystery yet unsolved. Here Gwador left his mark upon the land, burying the found miner's pick beneath the hollow of an old tree outside the Keep.

The Broken Hilt and the Whispered Deals

Back in Frandor’s Keep, the party sold their spoils, beaver pelts fetching a fair price, and the weapons they had taken donated to the Keep. But in the Broken Hilt Tavern, the night whispered with new intrigue.

Grond, the grizzled warrior, came to them, his tone softer than before. “I owe you an apology,” he admitted. “I was so damned furious about that blasted message board, I forgot why you even came to me. A week back, the caravan I was with was attacked by wolf-men.”

At that, ears perked. This was a trail they could follow. They agreed to journey with Grond and see where it led.

Lamar Vikara, master of the Broken Hilt, also had words for them. “There’s a line I don’t let people cross,” he murmured, his voice low and dangerous. “Soldiers fight out there, not under my roof. Some folk are testing the limits. Best they think twice before they do.”

And then: “You’ve been asking questions. Coin and drink loosen lips.”

Wolves, Silver, and Forgotten Shrines

On the morning of the twelfth day of Planting, the hunt began. Delvan sought silver to prepare for the coming battles, but the price was steep—100 gold pieces for a single silver blade. Greytar, the learned man, confirmed what they had feared: only silver or enchanted weapons could wound the creatures that stalked the night. And worse yet, the full moon was no shackle to their transformations.

Yet there was hope. Greytar spoke of a shrine—forgotten, hidden in the wilds, dedicated to Mayaheine. Within its sacred halls, holy weapons might still rest, waiting to be reclaimed. But it was a place of tomes as well, and Greytar had an interest in such things. The party struck a deal: should they find the books, they would return them.

The Caravan’s Ruin

With Grond leading the way, they followed the trail of a ruined caravan. On the journey, Grond shared whispers of his own:

  • Something was wrong at Vespin Tower. Soldiers had died, and the Prefect wished to keep it quiet.
  • Harga the blacksmith was not to be trusted.
  • Those locked out of the Keep at night could buy their way into the Palisade for a handful of silver.

At last, they found the wreckage. Smashed wagons, scattered goods, and then—the attack. Not wolf-men, but apes, unnatural in their fury. The battle was swift, brutal.

Fortune and Fire

Upon their return, they sold their spoils—silk and pelts fetching a fine price. Belipar, grateful, paid handsomely for his paint.

Yet even in victory, the road ahead grew darker. For wolves still prowled. Secrets still festered.

And in the distance, the forgotten shrine awaited.
