The Ballad of Frandor’s Keep: A Tale of Heroes and Shadows

By the wandering bard, Lirien the Silver-Tongued

Our Heros

  • Delvon Brightwood – Half-elf Paladin
  • Flint Sunsong – Dwarf Monk
  • Gimblebok – Gnome Druid
  • Gwador – Half-orc Fighter

The Chronicles of Frandor’s Keep: A Bard’s Tale

It was on the 8th of Planting, beneath skies bruised with the colors of twilight, that four wanderers found themselves treading the Borderland Road, each drawn by fate toward the weathered stone walls of Frandor’s Keep.

The first was Delvon Brightwood, a half-elven paladin, slender as a rapier but with a heart of tempered steel. The second, Flint Sunsong, a dwarven monk, moved with the silent precision of falling leaves, yet carried the weight of battle upon his knotted shoulders. The third, Gimblebok, a gnome druid, bore an untamed spirit, his keen eyes catching the flicker of injustice before most. And last, towering above the rest, was Gwador, a half-orc fighter, whose presence alone stirred old hatreds in the hearts of men.

They had not yet met, nor did they know that soon they would walk the same road, shed blood in the same battle, and forge bonds unbreakable. But the gods—and the whispers of the wind—knew otherwise.

Fangs in the Shadows & Bonds Forged in Steel

Upon arrival, Gimblebok’s gaze fell upon a caged mountain lion, its once-proud form shackled and defeated. When he inquired about the poor beast, the guards laughed cruelly. "Jiric Helsan’s bounty," they said, referring to the infamous broker of bloodsport, a man who paid handsomely for both beasts and men to fight in the gladiatorial pits of the Hold of the Sea Princes. The gnome’s heart burned with quiet fury, but he said nothing—yet.

Elsewhere, Delvon Brightwood, righteous in his bearing, was approached by a beggar named Shanko, a wretch of the road with more ghosts in his eyes than coin in his hand. The paladin, moved by pity, gifted the man a single gold piece. Shanko, lips trembling, whispered his warning:

"May the gods bless you. Now be careful around here, especially at night. Especially at night. I've seen things... I saw my buddy Thak ripped to threads by a man-wolf in Vell's Clearing."

The words clung to Delvon like a curse.

Meanwhile, Flint Sunsong, sharp-eyed and cautious, declined the service of a boy named Vic, who offered to guide him through the Keep and the wilds. A wise decision, perhaps, perhaps not—but destiny would not let him walk alone for long.

Then, a commotion.

The crowd parted like a wound in the street as Gwador, the half-orc, strode toward the Keep’s gate. He had done nothing but exist—yet his presence alone was enough to stir the venom of the guards.

"Where do you think you're going, mucksnout?" sneered the first, pressing steel against his chest.

"You’re lucky we ain’t still stacking your kind’s skulls along the wall," added the second.

The old hatred of men and orcs ran deep, but Gwador did not flinch. He did not snarl nor show his fangs, for he was no beast. Instead, he uttered the simple truth:

"I need work."

The guards mocked him, as cruel men do when they hold power, but it was then that Gimblebok, ever the clever one, strode past and, with a smirk, Gwador pointed at him.

"That one," the half-orc said, "is my boss."

A moment of stunned silence. The guards glanced at the tiny gnome, then at the towering warrior, then back again. Their minds, dulled by arrogance, could not grasp the absurdity of what was before them. And so, baffled into submission, they let them pass.

It was then that Delvon and Flint joined them, their fates now intertwined. Four strangers, bound by chance—or was it something more?

Secrets of the Keep

Within the Keep, the four met Randric, a guide with a tongue as quick as a gambler’s hands. For a price, he shared whispers carried on the wind:

  • A statue stolen from the Golden Lynx Gate.
  • Strange movements at Karapar Tower—perhaps a hidden silver strike.
  • Wolves circling the Keep... but not all wolves are what they seem.

More troubling still was the bounty board, where the Baron himself placed value upon death: a mere one gold piece per pair of humanoid ears. The implications sickened Delvon, but it was Gimblebok who vowed in hushed tones, "Jiric Helsan’s business ends now."

That night, the Broken Hilt Tavern swelled with voices, drink, and a hundred little intrigues. They met Grond, a mercenary duped by a dishonest scribe; they heard rumors of a witch in the woods, of a giant who hurled stones into the Keep, and of spies in service of the enigmatic Rikar.

Yet the greatest tale came from a soldier named Dvorn, who spoke of something terrible stalking the Borderland Road.

"It’s large," he whispered over his tankard. "Red eyes. Fangs like knives. I heard it dragging something through the brush... and whatever it was, it screamed like a man."

Blood Upon the Hell’s Throat Trail

The next morning, duty called them forth, for they had accepted a mission to investigate Vespin Tower, where the Keep’s garrison had lost contact with their men. Their journey led them through the infamous Hell’s Throat Trail, a road cursed by dark tales.

And there, beneath the shadowed canopy, they were ambushed.

A bugbear, hulking and brutal, struck from the darkness with savage precision. It might have been the end of them—but four blades are sharper than one. Though wounded, they felled the beast, and Delvon claimed its morningstar as his prize.

When at last they reached Vespin Tower, they were greeted by the stench of death. Two goblins lay splattered upon the earth, their bodies broken as if cast from the heavens. Scaling the tower, they found three more goblins within—two asleep, one awake.

They struck without mercy.

Within the tower, they found the corpses of two human guards—men who had died fighting for their lives. With solemn reverence, they buried the fallen, for even the forgotten deserved rest.

And thus, with blood upon their hands and mysteries upon their minds, they returned to Frandor’s Keep.

The Declaration

A grizzled mercenary named Grond raged over a scribe’s mistake, and in a moment of justice served through ink and parchment, Delvon and Flint ensured the man’s grievances were mended.

But it was in the Prancing Pegasus Inn where the most unsettling offer was whispered. Jiric Helsan—the man who trafficked lives as one might barter for grain—leaned close to Delvon, a smirk curling his lips.

"Why settle for a single coin for a pair of ears," he mused, "when you could claim three gold for a lion, thirty for an ogre, or ten for a werewolf—alive?"

The paladin’s fingers curled into a fist. His heart thundered with unspoken rage.

But when their paths crossed once more before the Bounty Master’s hall, the gnome spoke.

"This ends now," he declared.

A declaration. A warning.

Perhaps even a promise.
