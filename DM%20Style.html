Traps

This is an excerpt from A Quick Primer for Old School Gaming by Matthew J. Finch and Mythmere Games.

The Pit Trap

Modern Style

  • GM: “A ten-foot wide corridor leads north into the darkness.”
  • John the Rogue: “I check for traps.”
  • GM: “What’s your target number for checking?”
  • John the Rogue: “15.”
  • GM: Decides that the pit trap in front of the party is “standard,” so all John has to do is roll a 15 or better. “Roll a d20.”
  • John the Rogue: “16.”
  • GM: “Probing ahead of you, you find a thin crack in the floor – it looks like there’s a pit trap.”
  • John the Rogue: “Can I disarm it?”
  • GM: “What’s your target number for that?”
  • John the Rogue: “12. I rolled a 14.”
  • GM: “Okay, moving carefully, you’re able to jam the mechanism so the trap won’t open.”
  • John the Rogue: “We walk across. I go first.”

Old Style

  • GM: “A ten-foot wide corridor leads north into the darkness.”
  • John the Roguish: “We move forward, poking the floor ahead with our ten foot pole.”
  • GM: Is about to say that the pole pushes open a pit trap, when he remembers something. “Wait, you don’t have the ten foot pole any more. You fed it to the stone idol.” [if the party still had the pole, John would have detected the trap automatically]
  • John the Roguish: “I didn’t feed it to the idol, the idol ate it when I poked its head.”
  • GM: “That doesn’t mean you have the pole back. Do you go into the corridor?”
  • John the Roguish: “No. I’m suspicious. Can I see any cracks in the floor, maybe shaped in a square?”
  • GM: Mulls this over, because there’s a pit trap right where John is looking. But it’s dark, so “No, there are about a million cracks in the floor. You wouldn’t see a pit trap that easily, anyway.” [A different referee might absolutely decide that John sees the trap, since he’s looking in the right place for the right thing].
  • John the Roguish: “Okay. I take out my waterskin from my backpack. And I’m going to pour some water onto the floor. Does it trickle through the floor anywhere, or reveal some kind of pattern?”
  • GM: “Yeah, the water seems to be puddling a little bit around a square shape in the floor where the square is a little higher than the rest of the floor.”
  • John the Roguish: “Like there’s a covered pit trap?”
  • GM: “Could be.”
  • John the Roguish: “Can I disarm it?”
  • GM: “How?”
  • John the Roguish: “I don’t know, maybe make a die roll to jam the mechanism?”
  • GM: “You can’t see a mechanism. You step on it, there’s a hinge, you fall. What are you going to jam?”
  • John the Roguish: “I don’t know. Okay, let’s just walk around it.”
  • GM: “You walk around it, then. There’s about a two-foot clearance on each side.”

In an ideal world I would run the old style. I remember running it that way when I was a young whipper snapper. It was definitely more interesting and immersive.

But here's the issue. A smart party is going to be doing this everywhere. Including the 99% or whatever % of hallways/rooms that have no traps. I only have 4 hours twice a month to play. I want stories about adventures not Bomb Squad - these are their stories.

So you can rely on Passive Perception or state you want to "search for traps" - actively use Perception - in which case I will make a secret roll for the PC performing the actual search. If you succeed and I determine the trap is "interesting" I'll notifiy you that something feels off but you're not exactly sure what. And we'll shift to Old School mode.

By the way, I'm not a big trap guy, but they certainly have their place. Basically I prefer fewer interesting traps to losts of ordinary ones. However, I use published materials and while often I do remove traps from those materials I'm often left with more than I would normally have if I crafted my own.

Repopulation

If you've read enough about gamemastering you've propably come across the idea of after the players have cleared part of a lair, over time other creatures would repopulate that area. For example, in a multi-level dungeon the players clear out level 1 and decide to spend their hard earned coin and some downtime, to learn some tool proficiencies. During that time other creatures would likely move in the recently vacated level. This makes perfect sense. The players made a decision. Decision have consequences. I would love to run a campaign like this. It would feel real, lived in.

However, there's already a campaign setting that feels real and it's called real life. And it doesn't give two shits about your desires. And just like with traps, we have a story to tell! I can't spend time having the players clear the same level they've already cleared, now with different actors. So guess what? Despite being gone for sometime, the dungeon is exactly as the player's left it. Well maybe. Decision do have consequences. So don't be surprised if some travelling creature happened to find the treasure that the players didn't know was in the secret room.

Now in our Mount of the Red Door campaign during an extended downtime there was a story related reason where one level, the actually changed and was repopulated, but that is very rare occurance for me.
